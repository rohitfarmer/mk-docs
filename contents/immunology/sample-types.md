# Sample Types from Blood and Body Fluids

Researchers and clinicians often study different parts of blood and body fluids to understand health and disease. Some commonly used sample types are plasma, serum, PBMCs, and cerebrospinal fluid. Each sample contains different substances and is useful for different types of tests.

## Blood Plasma

Plasma is the liquid portion of blood. About 55 percent of blood is plasma, while the remaining 45 percent consists of red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets suspended in the plasma. Plasma is mostly water, about 92 percent.

Plasma carries many important substances throughout the body. It transports hormones, nutrients, proteins, and antibodies to different tissues. It also helps move oxygen and carbon dioxide between tissues and the lungs.

Plasma helps maintain blood pressure and supports normal blood circulation. It distributes water throughout the body and helps regulate body temperature by absorbing and releasing heat.

Another important function of plasma is removing waste products from cells. These wastes are transported to organs such as the liver, lungs, and kidneys so they can be removed from the body.

Plasma also contains clotting factors, which help stop bleeding when blood vessels are injured. In addition, plasma contains components of the immune system that help protect the body from infections caused by bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites.

To obtain plasma in the laboratory, blood is collected in a tube that prevents clotting. The sample is then centrifuged, which separates the plasma from the blood cells.

### Convalescent Plasma

Convalescent plasma is plasma collected from people who have recovered from an infection, such as COVID-19. This plasma may contain antibodies that recognize and fight the virus that caused the infection.

Because of these antibodies, convalescent plasma can sometimes be used as a treatment to help patients who are currently infected. The antibodies in the plasma may help the patient’s immune system fight the disease.

## Blood Serum

Serum is another liquid component of blood. It is similar to plasma but does not contain clotting factors.

Serum is the fluid that remains after blood has been allowed to clot. When blood clots, the clotting proteins such as fibrinogen are used up in the clot. The remaining clear liquid is called serum.

Serum contains many important substances including proteins that are not involved in clotting, electrolytes, antibodies, antigens, and hormones. It may also contain substances that enter the body from outside, such as drugs or microorganisms.

Serum does not contain red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets, or clotting proteins.

A simple way to describe the relationship between plasma and serum is:

Serum = Plasma − Fibrinogen

The study of serum is called serology. To obtain serum in the laboratory, a blood sample is first allowed to clot. The sample is then centrifuged to remove the clot and blood cells. The clear liquid left on top is the serum.

## Peripheral Blood Mononuclear Cells (PBMCs)

Peripheral blood mononuclear cells, commonly called PBMCs, are a group of immune cells found in blood. Unlike plasma and serum, PBMCs are cells rather than liquids.

PBMCs include several types of immune cells, such as lymphocytes and monocytes. Lymphocytes include T cells, B cells, and natural killer cells. These cells play key roles in the body’s immune response and help detect and fight infections.

PBMCs have round nuclei, which is why they are called mononuclear cells. They are important in studies of the immune system and are widely used in biomedical research.

PBMCs do not include granulocytes such as basophils, eosinophils, or neutrophils. They also do not include red blood cells.

Peripheral blood refers to the blood that circulates through the heart and blood vessels, including arteries, veins, and capillaries. Its main function is to transport oxygen and nutrients to cells and tissues and to remove carbon dioxide and other waste products from the body.

Because peripheral blood is exposed to substances such as drugs and chemicals, PBMCs are often used in research to study how these substances affect the immune system and the body.

In the laboratory, PBMCs are usually separated from whole blood using a method called density gradient centrifugation.

## Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF)

Cerebrospinal fluid, or CSF, is a clear and colorless liquid that flows around the brain and spinal cord.

CSF acts as a protective cushion for the brain and spinal cord. It helps protect these structures from injury caused by sudden movements or impacts. CSF also helps remove waste products from the brain and supports the normal functioning of the central nervous system.

Doctors and researchers often study CSF to diagnose diseases that affect the brain and spinal cord. This process is called CSF analysis.

A CSF analysis can help diagnose infections of the brain and spinal cord, such as meningitis and encephalitis. In these cases, tests look for white blood cells, bacteria, and other signs of infection in the fluid.

CSF analysis can also help diagnose autoimmune diseases such as Guillain-Barré syndrome and multiple sclerosis. These conditions may cause increased levels of certain proteins in the cerebrospinal fluid.

In addition, CSF tests can help detect bleeding in the brain, brain tumors, and cancers that have spread to the central nervous system. CSF analysis may also help doctors diagnose Alzheimer’s disease, the most common form of dementia, which causes memory loss, confusion, and changes in behavior.
