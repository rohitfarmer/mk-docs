# Antibodies and Types of Immunoglobulins

## What Are Antibodies

Antibodies are proteins made by B cells of the immune system. They help the body recognize and fight foreign substances such as viruses, bacteria, and toxins.

Another name for antibodies is immunoglobulins, often written as Ig.

Each antibody is designed to recognize a specific antigen. An antigen is a molecule from a pathogen that the immune system can detect.

When an antibody binds to an antigen, it can block the pathogen, mark it for destruction, or help other immune cells remove it from the body.

Antibodies are mainly produced by plasma cells, which are activated B cells that specialize in making large amounts of antibodies.



## Structure of an Antibody

Antibodies have a Y-shaped structure.

Each antibody is made of four protein chains:

* two heavy chains
* two light chains

These chains are connected by chemical bonds.

The top parts of the Y-shaped molecule are called the variable regions. These regions form the antigen-binding sites. This is the part of the antibody that recognizes and attaches to a specific antigen.

The bottom part of the antibody is called the constant region. This region determines how the antibody interacts with other parts of the immune system.

Because the variable region is different for each antibody, the immune system can recognize a very large number of different pathogens.



## How Antibodies Work

Antibodies help the immune system in several ways.

One important function is neutralization. Antibodies can bind to viruses or toxins and block them from entering or damaging cells.

Another function is opsonization. In this process, antibodies coat the pathogen and make it easier for immune cells such as macrophages and neutrophils to recognize and destroy it.

Antibodies can also activate the complement system. Complement proteins help break down pathogens and promote inflammation.

In addition, antibodies can cause pathogens to clump together. This process is called agglutination. Clumped pathogens are easier for immune cells to remove.



## Immunoglobulin Classes

There are five main classes of immunoglobulins in humans.

These classes are IgM, IgG, IgA, IgE, and IgD.

Each class has a different structure and performs different functions in the immune system.



### IgM

IgM is usually the first antibody produced during an infection.

It is often found in the blood and lymph. IgM molecules are usually arranged as five Y-shaped units joined together. This structure is called a pentamer.

Because of this structure, IgM can bind strongly to pathogens and is very effective at activating the complement system.

IgM is important during the early stage of an immune response before other antibody types are produced.



### IgG

IgG is the most abundant antibody in the blood.

It is produced later in the immune response after class-switch recombination in B cells.

IgG antibodies are very effective at neutralizing pathogens, activating complement, and helping immune cells destroy infected cells.

IgG is also important for long-term immunity. Many vaccines work by stimulating the production of IgG antibodies.

Another special feature of IgG is that it can cross the placenta from mother to fetus. This helps provide newborn babies with temporary protection against infections.



### IgA

IgA plays an important role in protecting mucosal surfaces.

Mucosal surfaces include areas such as the respiratory tract, digestive tract, and reproductive tract.

IgA is found in body fluids such as saliva, tears, mucus, and breast milk.

In these locations, IgA helps prevent pathogens from attaching to and entering body tissues.

IgA often exists as two antibody units joined together, forming a structure called a dimer.



### IgE

IgE is involved in allergic reactions and defense against parasites.

IgE binds strongly to special immune cells called mast cells and basophils.

When an allergen such as pollen or dust binds to IgE on these cells, it triggers the release of chemicals like histamine. This causes allergy symptoms such as itching, swelling, and inflammation.

IgE is also important for protecting the body against parasitic worms.



### IgD

IgD is less abundant than the other antibody types.

It is mainly found on the surface of naive B cells, where it functions as part of the B cell receptor.

IgD helps B cells recognize antigens and become activated.

Although its role is not as well understood as other antibody types, it is important in early stages of B cell activation.



## Antibody Diversity

The immune system can produce millions of different antibodies.

This diversity comes from several mechanisms during B cell development and activation.

One mechanism is V(D)J recombination, which rearranges antibody gene segments in developing B cells.

Another mechanism is somatic hypermutation, which introduces mutations in antibody genes during the germinal center reaction.

Together, these processes allow the immune system to recognize a very wide variety of pathogens.
